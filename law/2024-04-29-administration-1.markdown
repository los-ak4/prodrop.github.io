<div><h2>Anti-language    <span><span>[</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cant_(language)&action=edit&section=3" title="Edit section: Anti-language"><span>edit</span></a><span>]</span></span></h2></div>
<p>The concept of the <i>anti-language</i> was first defined and studied by the linguist <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Halliday" title="Michael Halliday">Michael Halliday</a>, who used the term to describe the <i><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> of an <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-society" title="Anti-society">anti-society</a>.<sup about="#mwt54"id="cite_ref-:02_10-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;:02&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-:02-10&quot;}}"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cant_(language)#cite_note-:02-10"><span><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></span></a></sup> An anti-society is a small, separate community intentionally created within a larger society as an alternative to or resistance of it.<sup about="#mwt55" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;:02&quot;}}"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cant_(language)#cite_note-:02-10"><span><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></span></a></sup> For example, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Podgórecki" title="Adam Podgórecki">Adam Podgórecki</a> studied one anti-society composed of Polish prisoners; Bhaktiprasad Mallik of Sanskrit College studied another composed of criminals in Calcutta.<sup about="#mwt56" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;:02&quot;}}"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cant_(language)#cite_note-:02-10"><span><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></span></a></sup></p>

<p>These societies develop anti-languages as a means to prevent outsiders from understanding their communication and as a manner of establishing a subculture that meets the needs of their alternative social structure.<sup about="#mwt59" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;:12&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-:12-11&quot;}}"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cant_(language)#cite_note-:12-11"><span><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></span></a></sup> Anti-languages differ from <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">slang</a> and jargon in that they are used solely among ostracized social groups, including prisoners,<sup about="#mwt62" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-12&quot;}}"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cant_(language)#cite_note-12"><span><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></span></a></sup> criminals, <span style="color:red">h</span><span style="color:orange">o</span><span style="color:#eed202">m</span><span style="color:green">o</span><span style="color:blue">s</span><span style="color:navy">e</span><span style="color:purple">x</span><span style="color:red">u</span><span style="color:orange">a</span><span style="color:#eed202">l</span><span style="color:green">s</span>,<sup about="#mwt63" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;:12&quot;}}"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cant_(language)#cite_note-:12-11"><span><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></span></a></sup> and teenagers.<sup about="#mwt66" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;autogenerated12&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-autogenerated12-13&quot;}}"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cant_(language)#cite_note-autogenerated12-13"><span><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></span></a></sup> Anti-languages use the same basic vocabulary and grammar as their native language in an unorthodox fashion. For example, anti-languages borrow words from other languages, create unconventional compounds, or utilize new suffixes for existing words. Anti-languages may also change words using <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metathesis_(linguistics)" title="Metathesis (linguistics)">metathesis</a>, reversal of sounds or letters (e.g., apple to <i>elppa</i>), or substituting their consonants.<sup about="#mwt67" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;:02&quot;}}"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cant_(language)#cite_note-:02-10"><span><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></span></a></sup> Therefore, anti-languages are distinct and unique and are not simply <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a> of existing languages.</p>

<p>In his essay "Anti-Language", Halliday synthesized the research of Thomas Harman, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Podgórecki" title="Adam Podgórecki">Adam Podgórecki</a>, and Bhaktiprasad Mallik to explore anti-languages and the connection between verbal communication and the maintenance of a social structure. For this reason, the study of anti-languages is both a study of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> and linguistics. Halliday's findings can be compiled as a list of nine criteria that a language must meet to be considered an anti-language:</p>

<ul><li>An anti-society is a society set up within another society as a conscious alternative to it.</li>
<li>Like the early records of the languages of exotic cultures, the information usually comes to us as word lists.</li>
<li>The simplest form taken by an anti-language is that of new words from old: it is a language relexicalised.</li>
<li>The principle is that of same grammar, different vocabulary.</li>
<li>Effective communication depends on exchanging meanings that are inaccessible to the layperson.</li>
<li>The anti-language is not just an optional extra; it is the fundamental element in the existence of the "second life" phenomenon.</li>
<li>The most important vehicle of reality-maintenance is conversation. All who employ this same form of communication are reality-maintaining others.</li>
<li>The anti-language is a vehicle of resocialisation.</li>
<li>There is continuity between language and anti-language.</li></ul>
